Component 1: Using the Main Library Search Bar (OneSearch)

The library search box acts like a giant search engine for our campus. It looks through books, articles, and videos all at once.

1Pick Keywords, Not Sentences

Do not type a whole question like "Should schools ban smartphones?" Instead, break your idea down into core word blocks connected by AND. Example: "smartphones" AND "schools".

2Use Filters to Clear the Clutter

Your first search will give you too many results. Click these buttons on the left menu to clean them up:

  • Peer-Reviewed / Scholarly: Limits your list to articles checked by real experts.
  • Publication Date: Change this to the last 3 to 5 years so your issue stays current.
  • Source Type: Check "Articles" or "News" to match what your teacher wants.

Component 2: Searching Dedicated Pro Databases

The main search bar is helpful, but going directly to a specific library tool gives you cleaner results with less junk.

Tool Name What It Does Best Quick Search Tip
Opposing Viewpoints Gives you pre-sorted pro and con articles on hot debates. Great for picking a side. Look at the "Viewpoint Articles" for ready-made arguments.
Academic Search Holds millions of deep academic papers. Perfect for finding heavy expert proof. Click the "Subject Terms" tags to discover better search words.
CQ Researcher Provides massive, neutral summary reports on single complex issues. Read the "Chronology" tab to see a clear timeline of the debate.

Component 3: Checking Free Web Sources (Google & Wikipedia)

You can use the regular web for your paper, but you must be a tough judge. Real research means verifying before you trust.

The Google Safety Checklist (S.I.F.T.)
  • Stop: Check the URL ending. Trust .edu (schools) and .gov (government) over .com storefronts.
  • Investigate: Look up the author. Are they a trained expert, or just an angry blogger?
  • Find Better Context: Check if other trusted news hubs say the exact same thing.
  • Trace Back: Trace the quotes or data links to see the original study yourself.
Using Wikipedia Smartly

Never cite Wikipedia in your final paper. Instead, treat it like a stepping stone to find the real sources:

  • Background: Read the introduction to learn basic names, vocabulary, and dates.
  • The Links: Scroll down to the bottom "References" box. Click those links to read the original source papers.
  • Talk Page: Click the "Talk" tab at the very top to see if editors are arguing over bad info or bias.

Component 4: Getting Citations & Quick MLA Guide

Every piece of data you borrow needs a citation. Let our library systems build them for you automatically.

★The One-Click Citation Helper

When you are looking at an article in EBSCO or Gale, look to the right-side toolbar. Click the "Cite" button, choose MLA 9th Edition, and copy it. Always fix formatting blunders like weird ALL-CAPS text before turning it in!

Standard MLA 9th Core Formula Structure:

Author Last, First. "Article Title." Journal Title, vol. X, no. Y, Year, pp. xx-xx. Database, URL.
